Gate of Memories · 역사의 장소
Seodaemun
Prison

Seodaemun Prison History Hall is a memorial museum and historic site in Seoul, South Korea, located within Seodaemun Independence Park. It preserves the prison complex built by Japan in 1908, which became a symbol of Korea’s struggle for independence and democracy. The site documents both colonial oppression and postwar political history through its preserved buildings and exhibitions.

History · 역사
History

Initially named Gyeongseong Prison, the facility was established by Japan’s colonial government to detain political dissidents. It later imprisoned thousands of Korean independence activists, including the teenage martyr Yu Gwan-sun, who died there after leading the 1919 March 1 Movement. After liberation in 1945, successive South Korean regimes used it for political prisoners until its closure in 1987.

Museum and exhibits

Opened to the public in 1998, the restored complex includes seven cell blocks, an execution building, interrogation rooms, watchtowers, and a memorial yard. Exhibits such as the "National Resistance Room" and "In-Prison Life Room" portray the hardships endured by detainees and their fight for national independence. Multimedia displays and guided tours are available in multiple languages.

Significance and remembrance

Designated a protected cultural property, Seodaemun Prison History Hall functions as a place of education and reflection on human rights, freedom, and Korea's modern history. Memorials within the park honor those who perished, reminding visitors of the resilience that shaped the Republic of Korea. The adjacent Seodaemun Independence Park also features the Independence Gate, Independence Hall, and monuments commemorating the March 1 Movement.
